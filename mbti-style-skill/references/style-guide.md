# MBTI IP Low-Poly Style Guide

## Style Diagnosis

The reference set is a cohesive low-poly cartoon IP system for MBTI personalities. The figures look like paper-cut or faceted 3D vector dolls: angular heads, wedge-shaped hair, triangular clothing panels, and simple prop-driven storytelling. The images are designed for app/UI use: high whitespace, centered subjects, readable silhouettes, light file weight, and strong icon recognizability.

Core style sentence:

> A flat low-poly geometric cartoon MBTI mascot, faceted paper-cut shapes, angular planes, simple expressive face, white studio background, soft grey oval shadow, bright but restrained palette, vector-clean edges, playful personality props.

## Visual DNA

### Geometry

- Build every object from large polygons: triangles, trapezoids, parallelograms, diamonds, and irregular quadrilaterals.
- Favor hard planar color changes over smooth gradients.
- Use faceted construction for hair, clothing, beards, tree canopies, furniture, and props.
- Keep hands and feet simplified into wedges or small blocky shapes.
- Make the head a faceted rectangular or pentagonal volume, not a round cartoon head.
- Use asymmetry: angled shoulders, one raised arm, tilted head, diagonal prop, or stepping foot.

### Character Proportions

- Head: large, about 25-35% of total body height for single figures.
- Body: compact torso with angular clothing panels.
- Limbs: thin, straight, slightly bent rods or tapered wedges.
- Feet: small triangular shoes or short block forms.
- Face: minimal dots, short lines, triangle nose plane, tiny mouth. Expression should be readable but not detailed.
- Hair: large solid faceted mass, often black, grey, yellow, teal, or purple; no strand detail.

### Line and Edge Treatment

- Do not use thick black outlines.
- Edges are created by adjacent color planes and crisp vector boundaries.
- If a contour line is needed, keep it very thin, dark, and rare; most separation should come from color contrast.
- Internal folds and facial marks are short straight lines.

### Color System

The set uses a limited palette per character, usually one dominant hue plus skin, grey, and one accent.

Core recurring families:

- Olive and forest green: `#407050`, `#709050`, `#A0C070`, `#409070`
- Teal and cyan: `#207070`, `#309090`, `#40A0A0`, `#50B090`, `#70D0D0`
- Purple and mauve: `#705060`, `#906080`, `#9070A0`, `#B080A0`
- Yellow and ochre: `#806000`, `#C09000`, `#D0B010`, `#E0C020`, `#D0E050`
- Skin and warm planes: `#E0C0A0`, `#C09080`, `#F0C0B0`
- Neutral planes: `#202020`, `#404040`, `#707070`, `#909090`, `#C0C0C0`, `#D0D0D0`

Palette rules:

- Use 5-8 visible colors total in a single character.
- Use 3-5 tonal planes inside the dominant hue to create the low-poly effect.
- Keep saturation moderate; colors should feel playful but not neon.
- Use white or near-white backgrounds so the character reads as a reusable UI asset.
- Reserve bright cyan/lime/yellow for glasses, butterflies, tools, or small accents.

### Light and Shadow

- Simulate form with flat light/dark facets, not realistic rendering.
- Use a top-left or front-left light assumption: left/top planes slightly lighter, lower/right planes darker.
- Add a soft grey oval or blurred polygon shadow under feet. Keep it light and small.
- Avoid cast shadows on the background except a faint floor shadow.
- Avoid glossy highlights, ambient occlusion, rim light, heavy drop shadow, or photographic depth.

### Composition

- Single characters: center the figure on a white square canvas, leave generous margins.
- Character should occupy about 65-85% of canvas height.
- Grounding shadow should sit below the feet, not become a platform.
- Small scenes: use a 4:3 or wide canvas, keep environment objects simple and low-detail.
- Props should be readable silhouettes: microphone, brush, palette, wand, ruler, laptop, chalkboard, bag, sword, umbrella, locker, flask.
- Use tiny speech/thought bubbles sparingly and avoid readable text unless requested.

### UI Design Implications

- The style is optimized for avatars, onboarding screens, MBTI result cards, app empty states, and sticker-like icons.
- Use clean whitespace and avoid full-bleed backgrounds.
- Keep each figure as a detachable asset that can sit on cards, result pages, or thumbnails.
- Do not put UI chrome around the character unless the user asks for an interface mockup.
- If adding labels, keep typography separate from the illustration; the original image language is mostly textless.

## Prompt Formula

Use this structure for image generation:

```text
Create a [format] of a [MBTI type or personality role] as a low-poly geometric cartoon IP mascot. The character is [pose/personality], wearing [simple faceted outfit], holding [one or two props]. Use crisp angular polygon planes, faceted hair and clothing, simple dot-and-line facial features, no thick outlines, flat vector-like shading, [dominant palette] with muted grey and warm skin tones, centered on a white background with a soft grey oval shadow. Make it look like a reusable MBTI app avatar, playful but clean, minimal scene detail.
```

Add a negative style clause when using image generation:

```text
Avoid photorealism, anime style, watercolor, oil paint, complex texture, thick black outline, glossy 3D render, realistic human anatomy, detailed background, heavy shadow, neon colors, text labels, logos, and overly cute chibi proportions.
```

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing an output, verify:

- The image is immediately recognizable as low-poly or faceted.
- The silhouette is angular and asymmetric.
- The face is simple: eyes, nose plane, mouth line, minimal eyebrows.
- The character uses one dominant color family with 3-5 tonal facets.
- Props communicate personality without clutter.
- There is no heavy outline, painterly texture, photorealistic anatomy, or detailed environment.
- The background is white or near-white.
- A soft grey oval or faint floor shadow grounds the figure.
- The image would still read clearly as a 128-256 px app avatar.

## Common Failure Modes

- Too round or chibi: sharpen the head, hair, clothing, hands, and feet into polygon planes.
- Too realistic: flatten shading, simplify face, remove anatomy details.
- Too busy: remove extra props, background objects, and text.
- Too modern flat-icon: add faceted light/dark planes to clothing, hair, and props.
- Too 3D glossy: remove gradients, specular highlights, and realistic cast shadows.
- Too dark: return to white background and moderate mid-tone palette.
